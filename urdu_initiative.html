Pakistan's linguistic landscape is diverse, shaped by Indic, Iranic, Turkic, and Western influences. The native languages of Pakistan include Pushtu, Panjabi, Baluchi, Sindhi, Saraiki, Balti, Shina, Gilgiti, and several others. Urdu is the country's national language, however, it is not native to the land. Historically, Urdu was brought to Pakistan by the Muhājirs ( مهاجرMuhājir (/mu-haː-jir/, مهاجر)

Urdu, which was their mother tongue, was pushed by the Muhājir elite to be declared the national language, as a means of cementing their influence over Pakistan’s governmental, bureaucratic, and economic institutions.†Some argue that Urdu was declared Pakistan's national language to reinforce the country’s Islamic identity, as the language had become closely associated with Muslim culture and traditions during British rule. This push for an Islamic identity, however, was also primarily championed by the Muhājirs, who, lacking ancestral ties to the land, strongly advocated for Pakistan’s national identity to be rooted in Islam rather than ethnicity. At the forefront of this effort were the country’s most influential religious parties by far—Jamāat-e-Islāmī (JI) and Jamīat Ulemā-e-Islām (JUI)—both of which were founded and led by Muhājirs. These parties sought to define Pakistan as an Islamic state governed by Sharia law and worked actively to suppress any ethnic narratives, frequently labeling them as anti-Islamic. Decades later, Urdu remains Pakistan’s official language. However, as a language introduced by Indian immigrants, it has largely alienated the majority of Pakistan’s native communities, who see it as foreign to their cultural and linguistic heritage. Efforts to align Urdu with Pakistan’s native languages by incorporating local elements have been rare and insufficient. Consequently, Urdu remains as disconnected from the land and its people today as it was at the time of its imposition. In fact, it has become even more detached through its rapid Anglicization. Meaning "Urdu initiative." Combines Urdu and the French word "initiative." )

Replacing Urdu as Pakistan's official language is fraught with risks. The last serious challenge to Urdu’s status led to the 1971 civil war and the loss of hundreds of thousands of lives—a path Pakistan cannot afford to tread again. A more pragmatic and peaceful approach to reclaiming our national language is to reform Urdu through a civilian-led effort, aligning it more closely with the linguistic heritage of Pakistan.

A national language must reflect the collective identity and aspirations of its people. Turkey’s post-Ottoman language reforms serve as a compelling example. By removing Persian and Arabic loanwords from the Turkish language and adopting a Latin script, Turkey established a language that was uniquely its own and symbolized its new progressive identity. In contrast, the Pakistani state has failed to reform Urdu to align with the identity and aspirations of its people. Saqāfat seeks to fill this void by advocating for civilian-led language reforms.
