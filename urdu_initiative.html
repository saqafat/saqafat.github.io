Pakistan's linguistic landscape is diverse, shaped by Indic, Iranic, Turkic, and Western influences. The native languages of Pakistan include Pushtu, Panjabi, Baluchi, Sindhi, Saraiki, Balti, Shina, Gilgiti, and several others. Urdu is the country's national language, however, it is not native to the land. Historically, Urdu was brought to Pakistan by the Muhājirs ( مهاجرMuhājir (/mu-haː-jir/, مهاجر)

Urdu Initiative ( اردو انیشیاتیو Urdu Inishyātīw (/ur-duː i-ni-sjaː-tiːw/, اردو انیشیاتیو)
Meaning "Urdu initiative." Combines Urdu and the French word "initiative." )

A national language should be a unifying force, embodying the collective identity and aspirations of a nation. Yet, in Pakistan, Urdu has been anything but. As a language brought in by Indian immigrants, Urdu's imposition alienated Pakistan’s native communities, their heritage, and their languages. It became a tool of exclusion, creating a divide between the ruling elite and the indigenous population. As a result, rather than fostering national unity, Urdu's imposition became a key catalyst of the 1971 civil war, which saw hundreds of thousands dead in its wake.

But what is the way forward? Replacing Urdu is neither simple nor practical. The power structures that entrenched it as the national language would fiercely resist any attempt to alter the status quo, as history has shown. More importantly, what would replace Urdu? Elevating any single ethnic language to national status would only deepen existing divisions, fostering further resentment among linguistic minorities. Additionally, the logistical and financial burden of replacing Urdu would be staggering, requiring billions to rework Pakistan’s education system, media, bureaucracy, and legal framework—an expense the country’s fragile economy cannot bear.

A more pragmatic and effective approach is not to discard Urdu but to reform it—to reshape it in a way that aligns it more closely with Pakistan’s native cultural and linguistic traditions. Language reforms aren't new -- after the dissolution of the Ottoman empire, the new state of Turkey undertook significant reforms to its national language in order to align it with its new identity. However, unlike Turkey, which sought to redefine itself in opposition to its imperial legacy through language reforms, Pakistan’s struggle is to define its identity in the first place. Therefore, Pakistan should under take Urdu reforms to not distance Urdu from its Persian and Arabic influences, as Turkey did, but rather to bring it closer to these influences to better reflect Pakistan’s native identity, situated at the intersection of Iranic, Turkic, Afghan, Indian, and Western influences.

Reforms for Localization of Urdu ( اصلاحات برائے مقامیّتِ اردو Islāhāt barā-e-Maqāmīyat-e-Urdū (/is-laː-haːt ba-raː-e ma-qaː-miː-yat-e ur-duː/, اصلاحات برائے مقامیّتِ اردو)
Meaning "Reforms for Localization of Urdu." Islāhāt (/is-laː-haːt/, اصلاحات) comes from Persian, which itself borrows it from Arabic إصلاح (iṣlāḥ), meaning "reform" or "correction." Barā-e (/ba-raː-e/, برائے) means "for," and Maqāmīyat (/ma-qaː-miː-yat/, مقامیّت) is Persian, borrowed from Arabic مقام (maqām), meaning "place" or "position." The term refers to reforms to adapt Urdu to Pakistan's native linguistic, cultural, and historical contexts while giving it a unique identity. )
