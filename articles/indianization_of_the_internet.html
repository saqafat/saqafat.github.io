The Indianization of the Internet (Written: Jan 28, 2025, Last Edited: Feb 1, 2025)

The internet is changing, and it’s becoming increasingly shaped by Indian cultural, historical, and political narratives. This shift—what I term the Indianization of the Internet—is the result of both unintentional and deliberate forces at play. On the unintentional side, it stems from India’s vast and rapidly expanding internet user base, as well as the disproportionate dominance of Indian professionals in the global tech industry, particularly in the United States. This overrepresentation allows the unconscious biases of Indian programmers, many of whom are sympathetic to Hindu nationalist ideologies, to dominate the systems and algorithms developed by major tech companies.

On the deliberate side, the Indian government has strategically mobilized its vast diaspora and technological resources to promote pro-India and Hindu nationalist narratives. This effort includes a sophisticated ecosystem of fake news websites, dubious NGOs, and social media troll networks designed to influence and manipulate public discourse both domestically and internationally.

This dual dynamic—unintentional algorithmic bias and deliberate propaganda—is reshaping the internet, embedding Indian perspectives and ideologies into the algorithms, platforms, and information systems that billions of people engage with every day. This article is the first attempt to compile and analyze these forces, explore their interactions, and document their impact on the target communities.

1.1.1. Pro-India Bias in Google's Platforms

Several instances have been noted as examples of pro-India bias in Google's search engine, where Indian cultural narratives appear to be prioritized, often at the expense of suppressing or marginalizing the identities and narratives of other cultures.

For example, when searching for terms such as "Punjabi" on Google Images (with the location set to the United States), the results predominantly show the Indian ethnic group known as Punjabis. In contrast, the Pakistani ethnic group, which is also known as Punjabis but has a distinct identity and culture, is entirely absent. This promotion of Indian cultural narratives while effectively marginalizing or erasing other cultures' identity is an example of pro-India algorithmic bias in Google's search engine.

Similar biases can be observed with other search terms as well. For instance, searches for "naan" on Google Images primarily display images and descriptions of Indian-style naan, despite naan being a type of bread consumed across multiple countries, each with its own unique variations and cultural significance. The same happens when the word "chai" (a term for tea in multiple languages) is searched.

A particularly concerning example of pro-India bias can be seen in Google search results for "Pakistan News" (with the location set to the United States), which are dominated by Indian news websites, despite India ranking lower than Pakistan in the 2024 World Press Freedom Index.[38]"Reporters Without Borders - RSF". https://rsf.org/en/index. Retrieved 2025-01-15. Moreover, Indian media outlets often appear at the top of search results, frequently outranking Western sources. This prioritization amplifies Indian perspectives while suppressing alternate narratives, potentially exposing American internet users to Indian propaganda (as discussed in the next section).

Additionally, instances of pro-India bias have been observed on YouTube, where Pakistani content in Urdu is frequently mislabeled as Hindi, leading to subtitles that are incomprehensible to Urdu speakers. This mischaracterization not only undermines Pakistan’s cultural contributions but also promotes an inaccurate association with India, misrepresenting the origins and context of the material.

These examples of pro-India bias align with broader concerns about algorithmic bias in Google's systems. 3. Data Poisoning Attacks

Several instances of uncoordinated data poisoning attacks by Indian internet users have been observed online. For example, searches for "Pakistani Punjabi" on Google Images often display several images of Indian Punjabis. This issue can be traced to the widespread mislabeling of images. For instance, some online sellers tag traditional Indian Punjabi clothing as "Pakistani," potentially to exploit search trends or attract unsuspecting buyers.[101]"Pakistani Designer Punjabi Suits Indian Readymade Salwar Kameez All Size Dresses". eBay. Retrieved 2025-01-15.[102]"Glamorous Indian Punjabi Suits Pkistani Salwar Kameez Kurta Shalwar Women Dress". eBay. Retrieved 2025-01-15. Google's algorithms, which rely heavily on website descriptions of images, consequently incorrectly categorize such images of Indian Punjabi culture as depicting Pakistani Punjabi culture. Such intentional or negligent misrepresentation of data to fool online systems promotes Indian cultural narratives on the internet while misrepresenting and undermining Pakistani culture and its identity.

Such data poisoning attacks are a growing concern as corrupted data infiltrates the datasets used to train AI systems. This contamination embeds misinformation and cultural biases into AI models, leading them to replicate these inaccuracies when served through the internet.[103]"Hackers, AI and the Risk from Deliberate Bias". World Economic Forum. 2018-10-23. Retrieved 2025-01-15. For instance, an AI model trained on images of Indian clothing mislabeled as Pakistani clothing would likely replicate these errors, producing Indian clothing when asked to generate images of Pakistani clothing.
