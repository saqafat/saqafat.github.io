ثقافت

Saqāfat (/sa-qaː-fat/, ثقافت )
Meaning "culture." Borrowed from Arabic ثقافة (meaning "culture" or "civilization").
  • میزوں
    Mēzõ (/mai-zɔ̃/, میزوں)
    Meaning "home" or "house." Borrowed from French maison (pronounced with a silent "n," /mɛ.zɔ̃/).
  • شناخت انیشیاتیو
    Shanākht Inishiyātīw (/ʃaː-naxt/ /i-ni-ʃi-yaː-tiːw/, شناخت انیشیاتیو)
    Meaning "Identity Initiative." The word "Shanākht" (/ʃaː-naxt/, شناخت) comes from Persian, meaning "identity," while "Inishiyātīw" (/i-ni-ʃi-yaː-tiːw/, انیشیاتیو) is borrowed from English, meaning "initiative."
  • مقالات
    Maqālāt (/ma-qa-laːt/, مقالات)
    Meaning "articles" or "essays." Borrowed from Arabic مقالات (plural of مقالة, meaning "article" or "essay").
  • رابطہ
    Rābiṭa (/rɑː-bi-ṭa/, رابطہ)
    Meaning "contact." Borrowed from Arabic رابط (meaning "connection" or "link").

Saqāfat (Urdu: /sa-qaː-fat/, "ثقافت ") is Pakistan’s Cultural Renaissance—a civilian-led movement to define who we are and who we want to be as a nation. For decades, successive governments have failed to take responsibility for this critical task. In their absence, others have stepped in to fill the void. Religious authorities push an Arabized identity, diasporic liberals reduce us to bland labels like “South Asians” or “Browns,” and our overly-obsessed keyboard-warrior neighbor to the east actively rewrites our narratives to fit their supremacist fantasies. This cannot continue.

History shows that national identity is built—it doesn’t form by accident. Turkey, after the dissolution of the Ottoman Empire, implemented deliberate cultural reforms to forge a distinct Turkish identity, distancing itself from its Ottoman past. Post-World War II Germany and Japan reshaped their cultural narratives to break away from their imperialist legacies and rebuild their nations on new foundations. Similarly, South Korea, after the partition of the Korean Peninsula, implemented reforms to establish a distinct national identity, setting itself apart from its northern kin.

Pakistan, however, remains trapped in the shadows of its past. Unfortunately for us, the government-led reforms to shape a national identity never happened—or never came through. In its absence, the country finds itself divided—fractured along ethnic, religious, and historical lines—leaving a vacuum for non-civilian forces to impose identities that serve their own interests. Saqāfat is a call for a national awakening—a citizen-led movement that challenges these imposed narratives and proposes reforms driven by the collective will of the people, without waiting for the government to act.
